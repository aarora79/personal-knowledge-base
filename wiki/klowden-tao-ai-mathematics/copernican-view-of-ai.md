# Copernican View of AI

The Copernican View of AI is Klowden and Tao's proposed long-term philosophical framework for human-AI coexistence, analogous to the astronomical revolution in which Earth was displaced from the center of the universe. In this view, human intelligence is not a uniquely privileged form of cognition but rather one "planet" of intelligence among many -- with artificial intelligences representing other planets that are comparable in some ways and distinct in others.

The authors introduce this framework by first surveying what they consider unsatisfying alternatives:

**Technical retreat.** One response to AI is to retreat into purely technical frameworks where philosophical questions become moot. In mathematics, this is the formalist view: manipulating symbols according to rules. In science, it is "shut up and calculate." In art, it is the artisan model: satisfying external parameters without judging value. In each case, no distinction need be made between human and AI work. The authors find this inadequate because technique does not capture the full experience of these disciplines or guide questions like how to motivate the next generation or what directions to pursue.

**Human chauvinism.** A second response is to ascribe ineffable special status to human intellect, permanently distinguishing human activity from AI replication regardless of technical achievement. AI becomes the "No True Scotsman" -- forever lacking true "soul" or "understanding." The authors find this inadequate because it risks degenerating into a "god of the gaps" philosophy where the list of qualities reserved for humans shrinks as AI capabilities grow. It also denies an uncomfortable truth: some human capabilities may be less sophisticated than we tell ourselves, and more mimickable by algorithms than we prefer to admit.

**Technological supersession.** A third response, favored by some AI enthusiasts, holds that human cognitive abilities will soon be completely superseded by AI, rendering philosophical concerns moot. Extreme versions view human intellect as undesirable tedium that should be replaced by automation to free up time for leisure. The authors note this risks civilizational degradation -- humans might become unable to monitor, control, or even understand the systems they have delegated their thinking to. They reference WALL-E as a cinematic illustration.

**The Copernican middle ground.** The authors draw on the history of astronomy for their preferred framework. Ancient cosmology was geocentric -- Earth had privileged ontological status at the center of the universe. The Copernican revolution dismantled this view, revealing Earth as one planet among countless others, receiving no preferential treatment from the fundamental laws of nature. Crucially, this disinterest from the universe does not contradict humans' strong investment in Earth -- we can still prioritize our planet while accepting that other planets exist and matter to their own inhabitants.

Applied to cognition: human intelligence has historically occupied the center of our cognitive universe. But as we encounter other forms of intelligence (in AI, and perhaps in ways we will continue to discover), we can accept that both human and artificial intelligences exist in the same ontological category -- with distinctive differences and complementarities. Our interests remain tied to the human intellectual sphere, but its relationship with other forms of intelligence can be explored for both practical purposes (achieving real-world objectives) and philosophical ones (gaining external perspective on human cognition).

The authors cite chess as a precedent. Chess was once the quintessential exercise of human intellect, but engines have outperformed human grandmasters for decades. Yet chess remains a thriving human activity: players use engines for training, revisit old theories, explore new ones, probe for weaknesses in invincible AI players, and create new forms of competition with varying AI assistance. The philosophical questions of what chess *is* and *why* humans play it remain worth asking, but the answers look nothing like the three extreme positions above.

## Key Points

- Long-term philosophical framework for human-AI coexistence
- Analogous to Copernican revolution in astronomy -- Earth not center of universe
- Rejects three alternatives: technical retreat, human chauvinism, technological supersession
- Human and artificial intelligences occupy same ontological category with distinct properties
- Prioritizing human concerns is consistent with accepting AI as comparable form of intelligence
- Chess provides practical precedent: engines dominate but game thrives with human/AI cooperation
- Enables both practical objectives and philosophical external perspective on human cognition
- The "god of the gaps" risk makes pure human-chauvinism philosophically unstable
