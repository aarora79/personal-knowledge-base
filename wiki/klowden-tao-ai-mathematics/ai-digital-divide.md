# AI Digital Divide

The AI Digital Divide is Klowden and Tao's term for the inequitable access to AI technologies that is emerging as these tools become central to research, work, and creativity. The paper identifies two layers of inequity -- a traditional divide between those with and without access to frontier AI, plus a more subtle second divide among those who do have access but are locked into specific models with uneven capabilities.

**First layer: access inequity.** Large-scale AI tools may only be available to well-financed or well-connected research groups, or to individuals who are most willing to hand over personal data and look past ethical concerns. Smaller institutions, developing countries, and individual researchers without institutional backing risk being left behind as AI becomes necessary for competitive research. This is the classic digital divide applied to AI infrastructure.

**Second layer: model fragmentation.** The authors identify a more nuanced problem: capitalized, privatized AI models competing for finite resources (investment, user base) are incentivized to develop "spiky" capabilities to maintain competitive advantage rather than provide consistent performance across domains. As individuals become locked into one model through institutional negotiations or market constraints, one model may give a decisive advantage over another in a particular research area, creating divisions even within the group that has reliable AI access.

The authors propose two complementary solutions:

**Smaller, targeted models.** Many research benefits do not require the most advanced frontier models. Smaller local models and non-LLM technologies (proof assistants) often return meaningful results faster and more efficiently than models requiring massive data centers. Distilling smaller models from larger ones with user-defined, targeted training libraries could allow a diverse array of community-maintained models to emerge as viable alternatives to corporate offerings. This is essentially the argument that has driven the success of open-source LLM communities like those around Llama, Mistral, and various specialized domain models.

**Public AI infrastructure.** The authors point to calls for regulatory action to preserve equitable access, including proposals for a "CERN for AI" -- a national or multinational publicly-funded AI research coalition providing public-facing resources. This would counter the current corporatized models that dominate the field and ensure individuals and groups who would otherwise be left behind have access to frontier capabilities.

The framing connects to the broader Faustian bargain concern: AI development optimized for profit concentration inevitably produces inequitable access. Redirecting development toward human benefit requires deliberate infrastructure investment and policy intervention.

## Key Points

- Two layers of inequity: access to any frontier AI, and fragmentation between competing models
- Corporate AI models incentivized for "spiky" capabilities to differentiate, not consistent performance
- Institutional lock-in means researchers get advantage/disadvantage based on which model they can access
- Smaller targeted models (distilled, local, open-source) can provide meaningful alternatives
- Non-LLM technologies (proof assistants) often outperform frontier models in specific domains
- Calls for "CERN for AI" -- publicly-funded international AI research infrastructure
- Redirecting AI toward equitable access requires deliberate policy and investment
