# Theory of the Firm

The theory of the firm, as originated by Ronald Coase in "The Nature of the Firm" (1937), addresses a deceptively simple question: why do firms exist at all? In standard economic theory, the price mechanism coordinates all resource allocation automatically. If markets work so well, why do we observe "islands of conscious power" (D. H. Robertson's phrase) -- organizations where an entrepreneur directs resources by authority rather than by price signals?

Coase's answer is that firms exist because using the price mechanism has costs. The firm represents an alternative coordination mechanism to the market. Within a firm, the entrepreneur-coordinator replaces the price mechanism: workers move between tasks not because of price differentials but because they are directed to do so. The firm is therefore defined by the supersession of the price mechanism. A factor of production enters an employment contract agreeing to obey the entrepreneur's directions within certain limits, in exchange for a certain remuneration.

Coase explicitly rejected several alternative explanations. He argued that the division of labor alone cannot explain firms, because the price mechanism already serves as an "integrating force" for specialized production. He also critiqued Professor Knight's theory that firms arise from uncertainty and risk-bearing. While Coase agreed that uncertainty is important, he noted that Knight's theory -- which emphasizes guaranteed wages versus residual income -- does not explain why the price mechanism is superseded. Entrepreneurs can buy advice, sell knowledge, or make contracts without directing production. The guaranteed-wage arrangement does not logically require direction of work.

The firm as Coase defines it closely matches the legal concept of the "master and servant" (employer and employee) relationship. The distinguishing characteristic is the right of the master to control the servant's work -- to tell them when and how to work. This contrasts with an independent contractor, who delivers results but is not directed in the process. Coase saw this correspondence between his economic definition and the existing legal framework as evidence that his theory was realistic.

Coase also distinguished between "initiative" (forecasting and making new contracts through the price mechanism) and "management" (reacting to price changes by rearranging factors of production under one's control). The typical business person combines both functions, but they are theoretically separable. The fact that they are usually combined is itself a consequence of transaction costs.

## Key Points
- Firms exist because the price mechanism has costs; firms are an alternative coordination mechanism
- The defining characteristic of a firm is the supersession of the price mechanism by entrepreneurial direction
- The employment contract substitutes one general agreement for many specific market contracts
- Division of labor and uncertainty alone do not adequately explain why firms exist
- The economic concept of the firm closely matches the legal concept of employer-employee
