# Price Mechanism vs. Entrepreneurial Coordination

Ronald Coase's 1937 paper identified a fundamental duality in how economic systems coordinate production: through the price mechanism (markets) and through entrepreneurial direction (firms). Standard economic theory describes a self-regulating system where supply adjusts to demand through price signals -- what Sir Arthur Salter described as a system that "works itself." But Coase observed that this description is conspicuously incomplete. Within firms, resource allocation follows a completely different logic.

Outside the firm, price movements direct production through a series of exchange transactions on the market. If factor A becomes more expensive in use X than in use Y, it moves from Y to X automatically as price differentials adjust. Inside the firm, this market structure is replaced by the entrepreneur-coordinator who directs production. A worker moves from department Y to department X not because of a change in relative prices but because he is ordered to do so. These two methods -- price coordination and entrepreneurial direction -- are alternative ways of organizing the same productive activities.

Coase drew on economists before him who had noted this duality without fully resolving it. Marshall introduced organization as a fourth factor of production. J. B. Clark assigned the coordinating function to the entrepreneur. Professor Knight introduced managers who coordinate. D. H. Robertson memorably described firms as "islands of conscious power in this ocean of unconscious co-operation like lumps of butter coagulating in a pail of buttermilk." But none had adequately explained why one method is used in some cases and the other in different cases.

The degree to which the price mechanism is superseded varies enormously across industries and firms. In a department store, space allocation might be done by authority or by competitive bidding. In the Lancashire cotton industry of Coase's day, a weaver could independently rent power, shop-room, and obtain materials on credit -- coordinating production almost entirely through the price mechanism. The amount of "vertical integration" varies from industry to industry.

The boundary between these two coordination methods is not fixed but responds to changes in costs. Government regulations can shift the boundary: a sales tax applies to market transactions but not to the same transactions organized within a firm, giving firms an incentive to internalize more activities. Similarly, quota schemes and price controls that do not apply to internal production encourage firm growth. Technological changes also matter: the telephone and telegraph, by reducing the cost of organizing across space, tend to increase firm size by making entrepreneurial coordination cheaper relative to market coordination.

## Key Points
- Two coordination mechanisms operate in economies: the price mechanism (markets) and entrepreneurial direction (firms)
- Within firms, authority replaces price signals as the allocation mechanism
- The boundary between market and firm coordination is not fixed but depends on relative costs
- Government regulation (taxes, quotas, price controls) can shift activities from market to firm coordination
- Technology that reduces communication costs tends to expand the scope of firm coordination
