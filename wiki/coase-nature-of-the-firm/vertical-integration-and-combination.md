# Vertical Integration and Combination

Ronald Coase's theory of the firm provides precise definitions for "combination" and "integration" -- terms that had been used loosely in economics. In his framework, a combination occurs when transactions previously organized by two or more entrepreneurs become organized by one. Integration (specifically vertical integration) is the special case where the combined transactions were previously conducted between the entrepreneurs on a market. A firm can expand through either or both mechanisms.

Vertical integration involves internalizing market transactions -- bringing within the firm activities that were previously coordinated through the price mechanism. When a manufacturer acquires a supplier, the exchange transactions between them (purchase orders, price negotiations, contract enforcement) are replaced by internal direction. The manufacturer's entrepreneur now directs the previously independent supplier's operations. This makes economic sense when the transaction costs of the market relationship exceed the costs of internal organization.

Coase illustrated the logic of multi-product firms using a spatial analogy inspired by von Thunen. Imagine a town as a consuming center with industries arranged in concentric rings. An entrepreneur starting in industry B finds that as he extends activities in B, organization costs rise. At some point, it becomes cheaper to organize exchange transactions in a nearby industry (A or C) than to organize further transactions in B. The firm therefore naturally comes to include more than one product. This demonstrates that an upward-sloping cost curve for a single product does not, by itself, limit firm size -- the firm can expand into adjacent products.

This insight challenged the prevailing analysis of firm size under both perfect and imperfect competition. Under perfect competition, economists assumed the upward-sloping cost curve limited the firm. Under imperfect competition, Mrs. Robinson assumed one product per firm. Coase argued both approaches were incomplete because they ignored the multi-product nature of real firms and the transaction cost logic that determines which products a firm produces.

The historical transition from domestic production systems to factory systems illustrates these dynamics. As Maurice Dobb noted, the factory system lost its rationale when bonded labor ended (because the price mechanism could coordinate independent workers), but regained it with the introduction of power machinery (which created efficiencies from physical concentration that made internal coordination cheaper than market coordination). The movement between organizational forms responds to changes in relative costs of market versus firm coordination.

## Key Points
- Combination: transactions previously organized by multiple entrepreneurs become organized by one
- Integration: specifically, previously market-based transactions between entrepreneurs are brought internal
- Firms expand into multiple products when organizing a new product becomes cheaper than further expanding the old
- Upward-sloping cost curves for individual products do not limit overall firm size
- Historical transitions between factory and domestic production systems reflect changing relative coordination costs
