# Knight's Uncertainty Theory of the Firm

Frank Knight's "Risk, Uncertainty and Profit" (1921) offered what was, before Coase, the most influential explanation for why firms exist. Coase engaged extensively with Knight's theory in "The Nature of the Firm" (1937), accepting some premises while arguing the theory was fundamentally incomplete.

Knight begins by imagining an economy without uncertainty, where individuals have perfect knowledge. In such an "Eden-like situation," there would be no need for responsible management or control of productive activity. Marketing transactions "in any realistic sense" would not occur -- the flow of resources to consumers would be entirely automatic. There might be managers for coordinating activities, but they would perform "a purely routine function, without responsibility of any sort."

The introduction of uncertainty changes everything. When people must act on opinion rather than knowledge, the "primary problem or function is deciding what to do and how to do it," while actual execution becomes secondary. This produces two key changes. First, goods are produced for a market based on impersonal prediction of wants, with producers taking responsibility for forecasting. Second, the work of forecasting and technological direction concentrates in a narrow class -- the entrepreneur. "Centralisation of this deciding and controlling function is imperative, a process of 'cephalisation' is inevitable."

The most fundamental change, in Knight's view, is the wage system: "the confident and venturesome assume the risk or insure the doubtful and timid by guaranteeing to the latter a specified income in return for an assignment of the actual results." The enterprise and wage system is "the direct result of the fact of uncertainty."

Coase's critique of Knight proceeds on several grounds. First, people with better judgment or knowledge can sell advice or make contracts based on superior forecasting without directing production. Second, much work is done to contract (where a contractor is guaranteed a sum for performing certain acts) without any direction involved. Third, even in Knight's hypothetical no-uncertainty world, coordinators would exist -- but Knight gives no explanation for who pays them or why. In essence, Knight explains why entrepreneurs receive residual income and workers receive fixed wages, but he never explains why the price mechanism is superseded. The mode of payment is irrelevant to the question of why an authority relationship (direction) replaces market exchange.

## Key Points
- Knight argued that uncertainty creates the need for entrepreneurs who bear risk and direct production
- In a world without uncertainty, coordination would be automatic and purely routine
- The wage system arises because the "confident and venturesome" guarantee income to the "doubtful and timid"
- Coase accepted that uncertainty matters but argued Knight's theory does not explain why firms supersede the price mechanism
- Superior knowledge can be monetized through advice or contracts without directing workers
- The mode of payment (fixed vs. residual income) is distinct from the question of authority and direction
