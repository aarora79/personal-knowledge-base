# Transaction Costs

Transaction costs are the costs incurred when conducting economic exchanges through the market's price mechanism, beyond the price of the good or service itself. Ronald Coase introduced this concept in his 1937 paper "The Nature of the Firm" as the central explanation for why firms exist. His key insight was that using the market is not free -- there are real costs to discovering prices, negotiating contracts, and enforcing agreements.

Coase identified several distinct categories of transaction costs. The most obvious is the cost of discovering relevant prices -- finding out what goods and services cost in the market. While specialists (such as brokers or market analysts) may emerge to sell this information, their existence reduces but does not eliminate these costs. A second major category is the cost of negotiating and concluding contracts for each individual exchange transaction. Even in markets with specialized cost-reduction techniques, such as produce exchanges, these costs persist.

A third and subtler category arises from the difficulty of writing complete long-term contracts. When a buyer contracts for a service over a long period, the difficulty of forecasting means it becomes impossible to specify in advance exactly what the supplier should do. The contract must therefore be expressed in general terms, with details to be decided later by the purchaser. This incompleteness of contracts is particularly important for labor services (as opposed to commodity purchases), because the range of possible actions is much broader.

Coase argued that firms emerge precisely to economize on these costs. Within a firm, the series of individual market contracts is replaced by a single employment contract in which a worker agrees to follow the entrepreneur's direction within agreed limits. The entrepreneur can then coordinate production without bearing the cost of negotiating a separate contract for each task. However, the firm must still carry out its coordination at a cost lower than the market alternative, because factors of production always retain the option of reverting to the open market.

The concept of transaction costs became foundational to the New Institutional Economics. Oliver Williamson later expanded on Coase's framework, identifying asset specificity, uncertainty, and frequency as key dimensions that determine the magnitude of transaction costs. Douglass North applied the concept to explain the institutional structure of economies more broadly. The transaction cost framework remains central to modern analyses of organizational boundaries, vertical integration, outsourcing decisions, and the theory of contracts.

## Key Points
- The cost of discovering relevant prices is the most obvious transaction cost
- Negotiating and concluding separate contracts for each exchange adds further costs
- Long-term contracts are inherently incomplete due to forecasting difficulty, especially for labor services
- Firms replace multiple market contracts with a single employment relationship to reduce these costs
- Transaction costs explain why not all economic activity is coordinated through the price mechanism
