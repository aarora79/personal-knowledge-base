# Firm Size Determinants

If firms exist because they reduce transaction costs, a natural question follows: why is not all production carried on by one big firm? Ronald Coase addressed this in "The Nature of the Firm" (1937) by identifying countervailing forces that limit firm growth, creating an equilibrium where both firms and markets coexist.

Coase identified three primary forces that limit firm size. First, as a firm gets larger, there are decreasing returns to the entrepreneur function -- the costs of organizing additional transactions within the firm rise. At some point, the cost of organizing one more transaction internally equals the cost of conducting it on the open market or having another entrepreneur organize it. Second, as organized transactions increase, the entrepreneur increasingly fails to place factors of production in their highest-value uses, leading to resource misallocation. Third, the supply price of factors of production may rise for larger firms, because the "other advantages" of working in small firms (such as independence and autonomy) may require large firms to pay a premium.

These factors produce a marginal equilibrium. A firm expands until the cost of organizing an extra transaction within the firm equals either the cost of organizing it in another firm or the cost of using the price mechanism. This is a direct application of Marshall's principle of substitution at the margin. The first two limiting factors correspond to what economists call "diminishing returns to management."

Coase further noted that costs of organizing increase with three dimensions: spatial distribution of transactions (geographic spread), dissimilarity of transactions (diverse activities), and probability of price changes (market volatility). This explains why firms tend to be concentrated geographically or functionally. It also explains why inventions that reduce spatial costs (telephone, telegraph) tend to increase firm size, and why improvements in managerial technique have the same effect.

Coase also resolved a paradox about multi-firm markets. If firm A and firm B each could organize a transaction between them at less than the current marketing cost, why does a market transaction persist? Because A taking over B's operations would mean A organizing all of B's processes -- and A's cost of organizing B's work will likely exceed B's own cost due to diminishing returns. The market transaction persists because each firm has reached its efficient scale.

Professor Knight had declared that the relationship between efficiency and size was "largely a matter of personality and historical accident rather than of intelligible general principles." Coase disagreed, arguing that his framework of transaction costs versus organization costs provided exactly those general principles.

## Key Points
- Decreasing returns to the entrepreneur function limit firm growth
- Resource misallocation increases as firms organize more diverse transactions
- Factor supply prices may rise for larger firms due to preference for small-firm independence
- Firm equilibrium occurs where marginal organization cost equals marginal market transaction cost
- Spatial distribution, transaction dissimilarity, and price volatility all increase organization costs
- Technology reducing communication costs tends to increase optimal firm size
